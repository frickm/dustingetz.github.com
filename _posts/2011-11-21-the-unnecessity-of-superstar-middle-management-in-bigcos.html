the [un]necessity of superstar middle management in bigcos 

 

"You may be content with the abstraction that management is simply incompetent. This is certainly a common sentiment. But how incompetent can the pointy-haired boss really be if he employs dozens of people? Shouldn’t a little startup using XP be able to wipe the floor with bad companies in an hour? If all it takes is better engineering practice, shouldn’t the better engineers always win?" [1] 

 

good article, this guy does affirm "management incompetent" opinion but does it well. but, i don't agree -- i think management is doing exactly what is expected of it. super-large software and systems integration contracts (think defense and aerospace) are risky, and no company is willing to accept the risk of a fixed-cost bid. the economics are such that almost all risk falls on the customer, and as such, bigco leadership can get away just fine, eeking out their 7% profit per hour billed, without superstar management and superstar technical teams. 

 

competition is helping this situation. For example, in 2011 Lockheed Martin has a corporate initiative to seek out and retain superstar talent, I heard they're only recruiting engineering college-hires at Stanford, MIT, Caltech. This is interesting because due to contract/project economics (big project = big hiring rampup, political balance changes every 4 years so funding shifts and projects get cut, internal job boards flooded, good people have to accept shitty assignments which really hurts retention). 

 

anyway, management is doing exactly its job. bigco isn't willing to pay for superstar middle management levels -- they are executing an established business model quite effectively without superstars, and it makes sense to continue to do that while its economically feasible.
